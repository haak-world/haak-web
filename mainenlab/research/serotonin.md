What does serotonin do? Our lab has been pursuing this question for fifteen years through electrophysiology, optogenetics, and computational modelling of the dorsal raphe nucleus — the brain's principal source of serotonin.

Early recording studies revealed that serotonin neurons fire phasic, heterogeneous responses to unexpected events during behaviour ([Ranade & Mainen, 2009](https://doi.org/10.1152/jn.91007.2008)). Optogenetic activation showed that serotonin is sufficient to change downstream processing: stimulation causes locomotor slowing and promotes patient waiting for delayed rewards, without being intrinsically reinforcing ([Fonseca et al., 2015](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2014.11.013); [Correia et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2017.07.038)). Recording from identified serotonin neurons during reversal learning demonstrated that these neurons encode unsigned prediction errors — responding to surprise regardless of valence — and adapt their cue responses far more slowly than dopamine neurons ([Matias et al., 2017](https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.20552)).

In a [recent preprint](https://doi.org/10.1101/2025.08.01.668048), brain-wide Neuropixels recordings combined with optogenetic stimulation revealed that serotonin modulates neural activity across thirteen brain regions in dimensions orthogonal to those encoding behavioural decisions — a mechanism we call orthogonal representational expansion. Current work across several ongoing projects is investigating whether serotonin neurons encode state prediction error — mismatch between the brain's world model and incoming evidence — and how this signal relates to novelty detection, learning, and the embodied investigatory behaviours through which animals actively sample their environment.
