Why HAAK

The system is its own experiment

Every platform you use was designed to extract something from you. Attention, data, value. The architecture is the politics.

HAAK starts from a different premise: what if the system that organizes your work was also the system that studies how work gets organized? What if every step was written down, indexed, auditable — not because someone might check, but because that’s how you build something you can actually steer?

The Invisible College

In 1646, Theodore Haak convened a group of natural philosophers who met informally to share observations, challenge each other’s reasoning, and build knowledge collectively. No institution chartered them. No platform hosted them. They organized themselves around a shared commitment to making their process visible.

Three centuries later, the tools changed but the problem didn’t. How do people coordinate intellectual work without surrendering control to the platform that hosts it?

What we’re building

HAAK is an externalized reasoning workspace. Everything that matters gets written to disk — research, review, strategy, architecture itself — into indexed, auditable structure. AI agents work alongside humans, assuming roles within explicit constitutions. The question at the center: where is human participation irreplaceable?

Not ideologically. Empirically.

We run experiments. We publish papers. The system accumulates evidence about its own operation. Architecture evolves through use. Methods sharpen through practice.

Three principles

Agentic. People and AI, working together with defined roles. Every action externalized as a situation trace — who did what, where, when, why.

Ontological. A formal structure for what exists and how things relate. Not hidden in model weights. Written down, so you can question it.

Constitutional. Rules you can read. Governance you can audit and change. The constitution belongs to the people it affects.

Why now

For the first time, an individual can direct something that functions like another mind. This is a genuine redistribution of intellectual power. The question is not whether to use it — it’s who decides how it works, and by what rules.

An open system, governed by its participants. Not a product to be sold. A commons to be built.
